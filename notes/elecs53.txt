### **1. Knowledge (Recall & Facts)**

**Definitions:**

* **Current Source:** Circuit providing constant continuous current independent of load impedance or voltage across it.
* **Current Mirror:** Circuit designed to copy (mirror) a reference current ($I_{REF}$) to an output terminal ($I_O$) using matched semiconductor devices.
* **Reference Current ($I_{REF}$):** The master programming current established by a power supply and a precision resistor.
* **Diode-Connected Transistor:** BJT with base and collector shorted (or FET with gate and drain shorted); acts as a two-terminal active diode establishing $V_{BE}$ (or $V_{GS}$).
* **Transistor Matching:** Fabrication of IC transistors on the same silicon die to ensure identical properties (identical $\beta$, $I_S$, $V_T$).
* **Current Steering:** Using a single master reference current to generate multiple scaled bias currents across an entire Integrated Circuit (IC).
* **Compliance Voltage:** Range of output voltage over which the current source behaves ideally (maintains constant current).
* **Early Effect (Base-Width Modulation):** Variation of output current due to changes in $V_{CE}$; physically limits the output impedance ($r_o$).

**Critical Numbers:**

* **Ideal Current Source Output Impedance ($R_{out}$):** $\infty \ \Omega$
* **Practical BJT Output Impedance ($r_o$):** $40\text{k}\Omega$ to $100\text{k}\Omega$ (typically $V_A / I_C$)
* **Basic Mirror Minimum Compliance Voltage:** $\approx 0.2\text{V}$ ($V_{CE(sat)}$)
* **Wilson/Cascode Mirror Compliance Voltage:** $\ge 1.4\text{V}$ (Requires at least $2V_{BE}$ plus saturation margins)
* **$V_{BE}$ Mismatch Impact:** $1\text{mV}$ mismatch in $V_{BE}$ between matched BJTs causes $\approx 4\%$ error in mirrored current.
* **Thermal Voltage ($V_T$):** $\approx 26\text{mV}$ at $300\text{K}$.

**Standards & Protocols:**

* **IEEE Std 315-1975:** Graphic symbols; standard circular symbol with an arrow pointing in the direction of conventional current flow for ideal independent sources.
* **JEDEC IC Fabrication Standards:** Process tolerances for matched monolithic pairs (e.g., MAT02, MAT12 discrete matched BJTs).

### **2. Comprehension (Concepts & Relationships)**

**Visual Interpretation:**

* **Ideal Current Source I-V Curve:** Flat, horizontal line parallel to the X-axis (Voltage). Slope $= 0$ (implying $R_{out} = \infty$).
* **Practical Current Source I-V Curve:** Slight upward slope in the active region. Slope $= 1/R_{out}$.
* **Basic BJT Mirror Schematic:** Two BJTs, bases tied together. Q1 is diode-connected (collector tied to base). Emitters tied to common rail (ground or $V_{EE}$).
* **Widlar Source Schematic:** Basic mirror but adds a small resistor ($R_E$) solely to the emitter of the output transistor (Q2).
* **Wilson Mirror Schematic:** Three-transistor configuration utilizing a negative feedback loop to drastically elevate output impedance.

**Pros vs. Cons:**

| Mirror Topology | Pros | Cons |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Basic Mirror** | Highly simple; requires minimum voltage headroom ($\sim 0.2\text{V}$). | High $\beta$ error ($2/\beta$ factor); low $R_{out}$ ($\sim r_o$). |
| **Widlar Source** | Generates stable microampere currents using small-value resistors. | $I_O$ highly sensitive to exact $R_E$ tolerance; non-linear scaling. |
| **Wilson Mirror** | Excellent accuracy ($\beta$ error squared); Extremely high $R_{out}$ ($\approx \beta r_o / 2$). | Requires higher compliance voltage ($>1.4\text{V}$); needs 3-4 transistors. |
| **Cascode Mirror** | Extremely high $R_{out}$ without feedback; excellent for FETs. | Significant headroom sacrifice; restricts maximum output voltage swing. |
| **MOSFET Mirror** | Zero gate current (no $\beta$ error equivalent); scales strictly by W/L geometry. | Threshold voltage ($V_{TH}$) matching poorer than BJT $V_{BE}$ matching. |

**Exam Traps:**

* **Assuming $\beta = \infty$:** In basic mirrors, $I_O$ is slightly less than $I_{REF}$ because $I_{REF}$ must supply the base currents for *both* transistors.
* **Ignoring the Early Voltage ($V_A$):** Assuming $I_O$ is perfectly flat. If $V_{CE2} > V_{CE1}$, $I_O$ will be *greater* than $I_{REF}$ due to the Early effect.
* **Discrete vs. Integrated:** Building current mirrors with discrete transistors on a breadboard fails due to thermal mismatches and $I_S$ variations. Mirrors only work precisely on monolithic ICs.
* **Widlar Equation Errors:** Forgetting that the Widlar formula relies on a transcendental equation (requires iterative solving or trial-and-error if $I_O$ is unknown).

**Bottom-Line Summary:**
Current mirrors and sources form the indispensable backbone of modern analog integrated circuit design, replacing bulky, unreliable resistive biasing networks with precise, thermally stable active loads and constant-current supplies. By leveraging the identical monolithic properties (matched $V_{BE}$, $\beta$, and $I_S$) of diode-connected reference transistors and their mirrored counterparts, engineers can steer exact current ratios across an entire microchip using a single master reference. While the basic two-transistor topology suffers from finite base-current theft and Early-effect output conductance, advanced architectures—such as the Widlar source for micro-current generation and the Wilson or Cascode topologies for massive output impedance—systematically eliminate these errors at the physical cost of increased silicon area and reduced voltage headroom.

### **3. Application (Calculations & Engineering)**

**Formulas:**

* **Reference Current ($I_{REF}$) - Basic Circuit:**

$$I_{REF} = \frac{V_{CC} - V_{BE(on)}}{R_{REF}}$$


* $V_{CC}$: Supply voltage (V)
* $V_{BE(on)}$: Diode-connected junction voltage ($\approx 0.7\text{V}$)
* $R_{REF}$: Programming resistor ($\Omega$)


* **Basic BJT Mirror Output Current (accounting for finite $\beta$):**

$$I_O = I_{REF} \left( \frac{1}{1 + \frac{2}{\beta}} \right)$$


* $\beta$: Current gain of the matched BJTs
* *Meaning:* Base current theft causes $I_O$ to be slightly less than $I_{REF}$.


* **Basic BJT Mirror Output Current (accounting for Early Effect):**

$$I_O \approx I_{REF} \left( 1 + \frac{V_{CE2} - V_{CE1}}{V_A} \right)$$


* $V_{CE1}, V_{CE2}$: Collector-emitter voltages of reference and output transistors (V)
* $V_A$: Early Voltage (V)


* **Area Scaling (BJT):**

$$I_O = I_{REF} \left( \frac{I_{S2}}{I_{S1}} \right) = I_{REF} \left( \frac{Area_2}{Area_1} \right)$$


* *Meaning:* Making the physical Emitter-Base area of Q2 larger scales the output current proportionately.


* **Widlar Current Source Design Equation:**

$$R_E = \frac{V_T}{I_O} \ln \left( \frac{I_{REF}}{I_O} \right)$$


* $V_T$: Thermal voltage ($\sim 26\text{mV}$)
* $R_E$: Degeneration resistor on the output emitter ($\Omega$)
* *Meaning:* Generates small $I_O$ from a larger $I_{REF}$ without requiring megohm resistors.


* **Basic MOSFET Mirror Output Current:**

$$I_O = I_{REF} \left( \frac{(W/L)_2}{(W/L)_1} \right)$$


* $W/L$: Width-to-Length ratio of the MOSFET channel
* *Meaning:* Strict geometric scaling; no base current error to account for.



**Practical Use:**

* **Operational Amplifiers:** Biasing internal differential pairs (e.g., the long-tail current source in a 741 Op-Amp).
* **Active Loads:** Using a current mirror instead of a collector resistor ($R_C$) to achieve astronomical voltage gains ($A_v = -g_m (r_{o1} \parallel r_{o2})$) while conserving silicon real estate.
* **Digital-to-Analog Converters (DACs):** Current-steering DAC architectures use scaled current mirrors switched in and out of the signal path.
* **Phase-Locked Loops (PLLs):** Charge pump circuits utilize matched current sources and sinks to charge/discharge the loop filter capacitor linearly.

**Design Trade-Offs:**

* **Output Impedance vs. Headroom:** The Cascode current mirror boasts massive output impedance ($R_{out} \approx g_m r_o^2$), providing near-ideal constant current, but consumes significant voltage headroom, preventing the amplifier output from swinging close to the power rails.
* **Accuracy vs. Silicon Area:** Adding a base-current compensation transistor (making a 3-transistor basic mirror) reduces the $\beta$ error from $2/\beta$ to $2/\beta^2$, but increases the required die size and parasitic capacitance.
* **Widlar $I_O$ vs. Component Footprint:** Generating $10\mu\text{A}$ from a $5\text{V}$ supply using standard Ohms law requires a massive $430\text{k}\Omega$ resistor (impossible to integrate on-chip cost-effectively). The Widlar source generates $10\mu\text{A}$ using a simple $1\text{mA}$ $I_{REF}$ and a tiny, easily integrable $11.9\text{k}\Omega$ resistor.

**High-Yield Exam Triggers:**

| Keyword / Visual Cue | Memory Shortcut / Translation | Typical Exam Question Format |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **"Diode-connected"** | Reference side of a mirror | Identify the component that establishes $V_{BE}$. |
| **"Bases tied, emitters grounded"** | Basic Current Mirror | Calculate $I_{REF}$ using $(V_{CC}-0.7)/R$, then $I_O \approx I_{REF}$. |
| **"Calculate exact current with $\beta=50$"** | Use $1 / (1 + 2/\beta)$ | Compute $I_O$ error due to base currents stealing from $I_{REF}$. |
| **"Microampere range" / "Small $R_E$"** | Widlar Current Source | Calculate required $R_E$ for a specific small $I_O$. |
| **"Early Voltage $V_A = 100V$"** | Include $(1 + \Delta V_{CE}/V_A)$ | Calculate the true $I_O$ due to output conductance slope. |
| **"$I_S$ is twice as large"** | $I_O$ is directly proportional to area | $I_O$ will be exactly $2 \times I_{REF}$. |
