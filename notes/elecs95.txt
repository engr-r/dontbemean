### **1. Knowledge (Recall & Facts)**

**Definitions:**

* **Steady-State Error ($e_{ss}$):** Difference between input and output as time approaches infinity.
* **System Type ($N$):** Number of pure integrators (poles at the origin, $s=0$) in the open-loop transfer function $G(s)H(s)$.
* **Position Error Constant ($K_p$):** Limit driving steady-state error for a **step** (position) input.
* **Velocity Error Constant ($K_v$):** Limit driving steady-state error for a **ramp** (velocity) input.
* **Acceleration Error Constant ($K_a$):** Limit driving steady-state error for a **parabolic** (acceleration) input.
* **Frequency Response:** Steady-state output behavior (magnitude and phase) to a sinusoidal input of varying frequency ($\omega$).
* **Bode Plot:** Logarithmic frequency response representation; Magnitude (dB) vs. $\log(\omega)$ and Phase (degrees) vs. $\log(\omega)$.
* **Nyquist Plot:** Polar plot mapping the open-loop transfer function $G(j\omega)H(j\omega)$ from $\omega = -\infty$ to $+\infty$.
* **Gain Crossover Frequency ($\omega_{gc}$):** Frequency where open-loop magnitude is exactly **$1$ ($0\text{ dB}$)**.
* **Phase Crossover Frequency ($\omega_{pc}$):** Frequency where open-loop phase is exactly **$-180^\circ$**.
* **Gain Margin (GM):** Additional open-loop gain allowable before system becomes unstable (measured at $\omega_{pc}$).
* **Phase Margin (PM):** Additional open-loop phase lag allowable before system becomes unstable (measured at $\omega_{gc}$).
* **Bandwidth (BW):** Frequency range where closed-loop magnitude drops by exactly **-3 dB** from its DC value.

**Critical Numbers:**

* **Ideal Phase Margin (PM):** **$45^\circ$ to $60^\circ$** (Balances fast response and acceptable overshoot).
* **Ideal Gain Margin (GM):** **$> 6\text{ dB}$**.
* **Decibel (dB) definition:** $20 \log_{10}(\text{Magnitude})$.
* **Single Pole Roll-off:** **-20 dB/decade** (or -6 dB/octave).
* **Single Zero Rise:** **+20 dB/decade** (or +6 dB/octave).
* **-3 dB Point:** Half-power point; voltage amplitude ratio = **$0.707$**.
* **Type 1 System limits:** $e_{ss} = 0$ (Step input), $e_{ss} = \text{finite}$ (Ramp input), $e_{ss} = \infty$ (Parabola input).

**Standards & Protocols:**

* **IEEE Std 100:** Standard Dictionary of Electrical and Electronics Terms (defines GM, PM, Bandwidth).
* **IEC 60050-351:** International Electrotechnical Vocabulary (standardizes frequency response and steady-state terminology).

### **2. Comprehension (Concepts & Relationships)**

**Visual Interpretation:**

* **Bode Magnitude Plot:** Straight-line asymptotes intersecting at "break frequencies" (corner frequencies). Slope changes dictate pole/zero presence.
* **Bode Phase Plot:** Asymptotic shifts. $-90^\circ$ shift per LHP pole; $+90^\circ$ shift per LHP zero.
* **Nyquist Plot:** Critical point for stability is **$(-1, j0)$**. Encirclements of this point determine closed-loop stability.
* **Nichols Chart:** Plots open-loop magnitude (dB) vs. open-loop phase on Cartesian coordinates; overlays constant closed-loop magnitude/phase contours.
* **Resonant Peak ($M_r$):** Maximum peak in closed-loop frequency response; directly correlates to transient overshoot ($\%OS$).

**Pros vs. Cons:**

| Analysis Method | Pros | Cons |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Steady-State Error Analysis** | Determines ultimate accuracy; easy to calculate algebraically using Final Value Theorem. | Zero insight into transient behavior or dynamic stability. |
| **Bode Plots** | Highly intuitive log-scale visualization; easy to sketch asymptotes; clearly displays GM/PM. | Fails for systems with Right-Half Plane (RHP) open-loop poles or dead-time without careful handling. |
| **Nyquist Plots** | Exact stability criterion for *any* system (including RHP poles and time delays). | Difficult to sketch by hand; unintuitive for extracting exact transient parameter impacts. |
| **Nichols Chart** | Direct conversion from open-loop to closed-loop frequency response parameters. | Extremely cluttered visual representation; rarely used without software. |

**Exam Traps:**

* **Open-Loop vs. Closed-Loop Transfer Functions:** Error constants ($K_p, K_v, K_a$) and Bode/Nyquist plots STRICTLY use the **open-loop** transfer function $G(s)H(s)$. Do not use $T(s)$.
* **Mixing Crossover Frequencies:** GM is calculated at **$\omega_{pc}$** (Phase Crossover). PM is calculated at **$\omega_{gc}$** (Gain Crossover).
* **Type vs. Order:** "System Type" = number of origin poles ($s=0$). "System Order" = total number of poles. They are NOT the same.
* **Stability Margins Sign Convention:** For a stable minimum-phase system, both GM and PM must be **positive**.
* **Non-Unity Feedback Error:** Standard $e_{ss}$ formulas ($1/K_v$, etc.) only apply to **unity feedback** systems ($H(s)=1$). For non-unity, calculate $E(s) = R(s) - C(s)$ directly.

**Bottom-Line Summary:**
Steady-state analysis and frequency response constitute the dual pillars of absolute system accuracy and relative stability evaluation in classical control engineering. By classifying a system's "Type" based on its open-loop integrators, engineers mathematically guarantee infinite-time tracking precision against step, ramp, or parabolic inputs using standardized error constants. Concurrently, frequency-domain mapping tools—Bode and Nyquist plots—translate dynamic open-loop sinusoidal responses into crucial stability metrics like Gain Margin and Phase Margin, explicitly visualizing the exact boundaries of system robustness and dictating the necessary compensator networks required to balance absolute tracking accuracy with non-oscillatory transient stability.

### **3. Application (Calculations & Engineering)**

**Formulas:**

* **Final Value Theorem (General $e_{ss}$):**

$$e_{ss} = \lim_{s \to 0} s E(s) = \lim_{s \to 0} \frac{s R(s)}{1 + G(s)H(s)}$$


* *Meaning:* Universal formula for steady-state error (requires stable closed-loop system).


* **Static Error Constants (Unity Feedback):**
* Position: 
$$K_p = \lim_{s \to 0} G(s)$$


* Velocity: 
$$K_v = \lim_{s \to 0} s G(s)$$


* Acceleration: 
$$K_a = \lim_{s \to 0} s^2 G(s)$$




* **Steady-State Error vs. Input (Unity Feedback):**
* Step Input ($R(s) = 1/s$): 
$$e_{ss} = \frac{1}{1 + K_p}$$


* Ramp Input ($R(s) = 1/s^2$): 
$$e_{ss} = \frac{1}{K_v}$$


* Parabolic Input ($R(s) = 1/s^3$): 
$$e_{ss} = \frac{1}{K_a}$$




* **Phase Margin (PM):**

$$PM = 180^\circ + \angle G(j\omega_{gc})H(j\omega_{gc})$$


* *Meaning:* Angular distance from instability boundary.


* **Gain Margin (GM):**

$$GM = -20 \log_{10} |G(j\omega_{pc})H(j\omega_{pc})| \text{ [in dB]}$$


* *Meaning:* Amplitude multiplier required to drive system to instability.


* **Nyquist Stability Criterion:**

$$Z = N + P$$


* $Z$: Number of closed-loop poles in RHP (must be $0$ for stability).
* $N$: Number of clockwise encirclements of $(-1, j0)$.
* $P$: Number of open-loop poles in RHP.


* **Frequency/Transient Correlation (2nd Order Approximation):**
* Resonant Frequency: 
$$\omega_r = \omega_n \sqrt{1 - 2\zeta^2} \quad (\text{Valid for } \zeta < 0.707)$$


* Resonant Peak: 
$$M_r = \frac{1}{2\zeta \sqrt{1 - \zeta^2}}$$


* Phase Margin / Damping Approx: 
$$PM \approx 100 \zeta \text{ (degrees)}$$





**Practical Use:**

* **CNC Machine Tools:** Mandating Type 2 systems to ensure zero steady-state error when tracking linear cutting ramps (constant velocity).
* **Audio Amplifiers:** Utilizing Bode plots to ensure perfectly flat frequency magnitude response and linear phase shift from $20\text{ Hz}$ to $20\text{ kHz}$.
* **Power Supply Regulation:** Tuning feedback loops to achieve $> 45^\circ$ Phase Margin to prevent voltage output ringing during sudden load transients.

**Design Trade-Offs:**

* **Accuracy vs. Stability:** Increasing open-loop DC gain ($K$) mathematically eliminates steady-state error but shifts the Bode magnitude plot upward, increasing $\omega_{gc}$, which degrades Phase Margin and pushes the system toward instability.
* **Bandwidth vs. Noise Immunity:** Expanding system bandwidth yields faster rise times and transient tracking, but simultaneously amplifies high-frequency sensor noise and resonance spikes.
* **Integrators vs. Phase Lag:** Adding a pure integrator ($1/s$) upgrades the System Type (eliminating $e_{ss}$ for higher-order inputs) but introduces an immediate $-90^\circ$ phase penalty at all frequencies, severely compromising Phase Margin.

**High-Yield Exam Triggers:**

| Keyword / Visual Cue | Memory Shortcut / Translation | Typical Exam Question Format |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **"Type 1 system, step input"** | $e_{ss} = 0$ | Calculate the steady state error. (Ans: $0$). |
| **"Calculate $K_v$"** | $K_v = \lim_{s\to 0} s G(s)$ | Find velocity error constant; multiply transfer function by $s$ and set $s=0$. |
| **"Encirclements of -1+j0"** | Nyquist Criterion ($Z = N + P$) | Determine closed-loop stability given open-loop RHP poles and plot encirclements. |
| **"Magnitude is $1$ / $0\text{ dB}$"** | Gain Crossover Frequency ($\omega_{gc}$) | Identify the frequency needed to calculate Phase Margin. |
| **"Phase is $-180^\circ$"** | Phase Crossover Frequency ($\omega_{pc}$) | Identify the frequency needed to calculate Gain Margin. |
| **"Increase DC gain $K$"** | $e_{ss} \downarrow$, GM $\downarrow$, PM $\downarrow$ | Conceptual question on the effect of increasing proportional gain on steady-state and stability. |
