### **Topic: Electronic Systems and Design - SCRs, UJTs, and PUTs**

---

### **1. Knowledge (Recall & Facts)**

**Definitions**

* **Thyristor**: Broad family of 4-layer (**PNPN**) solid-state semiconductor devices acting as bistable switches.
* **Silicon Controlled Rectifier (SCR)**: 4-layer, 3-junction ($J_1$, $J_2$, $J_3$), 3-terminal (**Anode, Cathode, Gate**) unidirectional thyristor.
* **Unijunction Transistor (UJT)**: 2-layer (**PN**), 1-junction, 3-terminal (**Emitter, Base 1, Base 2**) device exhibiting **negative resistance**.
* **Programmable Unijunction Transistor (PUT)**: 4-layer (**PNPN**), 3-terminal (**Anode, Cathode, Gate**) thyristor functionally acting like a UJT, but triggering parameters are user-configurable.
* **Holding Current ($I_H$)**: Minimum **anode current** required to maintain conduction after turning ON.
* **Latching Current ($I_L$)**: Minimum **anode current** required to successfully turn ON and stay ON after gate pulse removal.
* **Commutation**: Process of turning OFF an SCR (forcing anode current below $I_H$).
* **Breakover Voltage ($V_{BO}$)**: Forward voltage at which SCR turns ON without gate current.
* **Negative Resistance Region**: UJT characteristic where increasing current results in decreasing voltage.

**Critical Numbers**

* **Latching vs. Holding Ratio**: $I_L \approx (2 \text{ to } 3) \times I_H$
* **UJT Intrinsic Standoff Ratio ($\eta$)**: Typically 0.5 to 0.82
* **UJT Interbase Resistance ($R_{BB}$)**: Typically $4\text{ k}\Omega$ to $10\text{ k}\Omega$
* **PN Junction Diode Drop ($V_D$)**: $\approx 0.7\text{ V}$ (Silicon)
* **Firing Angle ($\alpha$) Range for SCR**: 0° to 180°

**Standards & Protocols**

* **JEDEC Standard No. 77**: Standardized nomenclature, symbols, and letter symbols for discrete semiconductor devices (thyristors).
* **IEC 60747-6**: International standard specifying discrete semiconductor devices (specifically thyristors).

---

### **2. Comprehension (Concepts & Relationships)**

**Visual Interpretation**

* **SCR V-I Curve**: Three distinct states—**Reverse Blocking** (avalanche breakdown region), **Forward Blocking** (leakage current only), **Forward Conduction** (triggered by gate or exceeding $V_{BO}$).
* **Two-Transistor Analogy (SCR)**: Interconnected **PNP** and **NPN** BJTs. Collector of each feeds the base of the other creating a **regenerative feedback loop**.
* **UJT Emitter Characteristic Curve**: Rises to **Peak Point** ($V_P$, $I_P$), drops into **Negative Resistance Region**, settles into **Valley Point** ($V_V$, $I_V$), and enters **Saturation Region**.
* **PUT Symbol vs. SCR Symbol**: PUT gate is connected to the **anode side** (N-layer); SCR gate is connected to the **cathode side** (P-layer).

**Pros vs. Cons**

| Device | Advantages | Disadvantages |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **SCR** | High voltage/current handling; extremely low forward voltage drop; fast switching. | Unidirectional (DC only or half-cycle AC); difficult to turn off (requires commutation circuits). |
| **UJT** | Simple oscillator designs; highly stable triggering parameters; low cost. | Fixed standoff ratio ($\eta$); very low power handling; obsolete in some modern designs. |
| **PUT** | Programmable $\eta$ and $V_P$; faster switching than UJT; operates at lower voltages. | Requires external voltage divider (extra components); susceptible to false triggering from noise. |

**Exam Traps**

* **Latching vs. Holding Trap**: Latching is for **Turn ON**. Holding is for **Turn OFF**. (Remember: *Latch to start, Hold to stay*).
* **UJT Terminal Confusion**: UJT has **no gate**. It uses Emitter ($E$), Base 1 ($B_1$), and Base 2 ($B_2$).
* **PUT vs. UJT Operation**: PUT triggers when Anode voltage exceeds Gate voltage by $V_D$. UJT triggers when Emitter voltage exceeds internal $V_P$.
* **SCR Turn-Off Fallacy**: Grounding the gate **does not** turn off a conducting SCR. Anode current must drop below $I_H$.
* **$dv/dt$ False Triggering**: Rapid voltage changes across SCR anode-cathode can charge junction capacitance, causing unintended turn-ON. Fixed by **RC Snubber**.

**Bottom-Line Summary**
Thyristors and unijunction devices form the backbone of industrial power control and timing. The SCR acts as a rugged, high-power, unidirectional switch triggered by a low-power gate pulse and sustained by regenerative internal feedback until forcefully commutated. Conversely, the UJT and its highly adaptable variant, the PUT, exploit unique negative-resistance characteristics to serve as precise timing and triggering sources for power devices. Mastery of these components relies on distinguishing their triggering conditions—specifically latching versus holding currents for SCRs, and peak-point threshold calculations dictated by the intrinsic standoff ratio for UJTs and PUTs.

### **3. Application (Calculations & Engineering)**

**Formulas**

* **UJT Interbase Resistance**:
$R_{BB} = R_{B1} + R_{B2}$
* $R_{BB}$: Total internal resistance ($\Omega$)
* Meaning: Resistance between $B_1$ and $B_2$ with Emitter open.


* **Intrinsic Standoff Ratio (UJT)**:
$\eta = \frac{R_{B1}}{R_{B1} + R_{B2}}$
* $\eta$: Dimensionless ratio
* Meaning: Voltage divider fraction internal to the UJT.


* **UJT Trigger (Peak) Voltage**:
$V_P = \eta V_{BB} + V_D$
* $V_P$: Peak voltage required at Emitter to turn ON (V)
* $V_{BB}$: Interbase supply voltage (V)
* $V_D$: Forward diode drop (typically 0.7 V)


* **PUT Programmable Standoff Ratio**:
$\eta = \frac{R_1}{R_1 + R_2}$
* $R_1, R_2$: External voltage divider resistors ($\Omega$)


* **PUT Trigger Voltage**:
$V_P = \eta V_S + V_D$
* $V_S$: Supply voltage to the external divider (V)


* **UJT Relaxation Oscillator Frequency**:
$f \approx \frac{1}{R_T C_T \ln(\frac{1}{1 - \eta})}$
* $f$: Frequency of oscillation (Hz)
* $R_T$: Timing resistor ($\Omega$)
* $C_T$: Timing capacitor (F)


* **SCR Conduction Angle**:
$\theta_C = \pi - \alpha$
* $\theta_C$: Conduction angle (radians or degrees)
* $\alpha$: Firing angle (delay angle before triggering)



**Practical Use**

* **SCR**: AC phase control circuits, motor speed drives, **crowbar overvoltage protection**, high-power welding controls.
* **UJT**: **Relaxation oscillators**, precise timer circuits, synchronized triggering of SCRs/TRIACs.
* **PUT**: Long-duration timers, low-frequency oscillators, battery chargers.

**Design Trade-Offs**

* **Oscillator Resistor Limits ($R_T$)**: Must be small enough to allow Peak Current ($I_P$) to flow ($R_{MAX}$ constraint), but large enough to prevent holding in the valley region ($R_{MIN}$ constraint).
* **$di/dt$ Protection (SCR)**: Rapid current rise destroys the die. Trade-off: Adding a series **inductor** protects the SCR but increases circuit size/cost.
* **$dv/dt$ Protection (SCR)**: High voltage rise rates cause false triggering. Trade-off: Adding a parallel **RC snubber** prevents this but creates leakage paths and dissipates heat.

### **Topic: Electronic Systems and Design - TRIAC, DIAC, and Other Thyristors**

---

### **1. Knowledge (Recall & Facts)**

**Definitions**

* **TRIAC (Triode for Alternating Current)**: 5-layer, 3-terminal (**MT1, MT2, Gate**), bidirectional thyristor; functionally equivalent to two inverse-parallel SCRs with a common gate.
* **DIAC (Diode for Alternating Current)**: 3-layer (or 5-layer), 2-terminal (**MT1, MT2**), bidirectional trigger diode; lacks a gate terminal.
* **Gate Turn-Off Thyristor (GTO)**: 4-layer, 3-terminal thyristor; turned ON by positive gate current, turned OFF by **negative gate current pulse**.
* **Silicon Controlled Switch (SCS)**: 4-layer, 4-terminal (**Anode, Cathode, Anode Gate, Cathode Gate**) low-power thyristor.
* **Light Activated SCR (LASCR)**: 4-layer thyristor triggered by **photons** (light striking the exposed silicon junction).
* **Shockley Diode**: 4-layer (**PNPN**), 2-terminal (**Anode, Cathode**) unidirectional trigger diode (SCR without a gate).
* **Reverse Conducting Thyristor (RCT)**: SCR with a built-in anti-parallel diode for freewheeling.
* **MOS-Controlled Thyristor (MCT)**: Thyristor switched by MOSFET gates; voltage-controlled rather than current-controlled.

**Critical Numbers**

* **DIAC Breakover Voltage ($V_{BO}$)**: Typically **$\approx 28 \text{ V to } 36 \text{ V}$** (symmetrical in both directions).
* **GTO Turn-Off Gain ($\beta_{OFF}$)**: Typically **$3 \text{ to } 5$** (requires massive negative gate current; e.g., turning off $1000\text{ A}$ requires $\approx 200\text{ A}$ to $330\text{ A}$ reverse gate current).
* **TRIAC Quadrant Sensitivity**: Most sensitive in **Quadrants I and III**; least sensitive in **Quadrant IV**.
* **Operating Frequencies**: Standard TRIACs/DIACs limited to low frequencies (**$50\text{ Hz } / 60\text{ Hz}$** or up to $400\text{ Hz}$); GTOs up to **$\approx 2 \text{ kHz}$**.

**Standards & Protocols**

* **IEC 60747-6**: International standard for discrete semiconductor thyristors.
* **JEDEC TO-220 / TO-92 / DPAK**: Common standardized thyristor package outlines for board-level design.
* **IEEE 519**: Harmonic control guidelines (highly relevant for phase-controlled TRIAC circuits generating harmonic distortion).

---

### **2. Comprehension (Concepts & Relationships)**

**Visual Interpretation**

* **TRIAC V-I Curve**: Symmetrical operation in **First** (Forward Blocking/Conduction) and **Third** (Reverse Blocking/Conduction) quadrants.
* **DIAC V-I Curve**: "Z" shaped symmetrical curve; blocks current until $V_{BO}$ is reached, then snaps back into negative resistance region before conduction.
* **GTO Symbol**: Standard thyristor symbol but with a **crossbar on the gate** (or two arrows pointing opposite directions) indicating bidirectional gate current capability.
* **SCS Symbol**: Standard thyristor symbol with **two gates** emerging from alternating layers.

**Pros vs. Cons**

| Device | Advantages | Disadvantages |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **TRIAC** | Single device for full-wave AC control; simpler gate circuitry than two SCRs. | Lower $dv/dt$ immunity; asymmetrical firing; lower power rating than SCRs. |
| **DIAC** | Symmetrical triggering; prevents TRIAC gate mismatch; extremely simple. | Very low power handling; purely a triggering/timing device. |
| **GTO** | Forced commutation not required (turns off via gate); high voltage/current. | Massive negative gate current needed for turn-off; complex gate drive circuit. |
| **SCS** | High flexibility (can be triggered/turned off via either gate). | Limited strictly to low-power / low-current applications. |

**Exam Traps**

* **DIAC Nomenclature Trap**: A DIAC is called a "diode" but behaves nothing like a standard PN junction; it is an **AC avalanche switch**.
* **TRIAC Terminal Trap**: TRIACs do **not** have an Anode/Cathode. They use **Main Terminal 1 (MT1)** and **Main Terminal 2 (MT2)**. Gate voltage is always referenced to **MT1**.
* **TRIAC Commutation Trap**: TRIACs cannot be turned off by the gate. They naturally commutate at the **AC zero-crossing**.
* **SCS vs. SCR Trap**: SCS has **two gates** (Anode Gate turns OFF positive current, Cathode Gate turns ON). SCR has one.

**Bottom-Line Summary**
While the standard SCR dominates high-power unidirectional DC switching, bidirectional AC systems and specialized control architectures rely on the TRIAC, DIAC, and advanced thyristor variants. The TRIAC, typically paired with a DIAC to ensure symmetrical firing, forms the cornerstone of commercial AC phase-control applications like light dimmers and motor speed regulators. Conversely, industrial high-power applications requiring forced turn-off capabilities without bulky commutation circuits deploy GTOs or MCTs, sacrificing gate-drive simplicity for robust, switchable high-voltage control.

### **3. Application (Calculations & Engineering)**

**Formulas**

* **RMS Output Voltage (Full-Wave TRIAC Phase Control)**:
$V_{RMS} = V_{S} \sqrt{\frac{1}{\pi} \left( \pi - \alpha + \frac{\sin(2\alpha)}{2} \right)}$
* $V_{RMS}$: Load RMS voltage ($\text{V}$)
* $V_{S}$: Source RMS voltage ($\text{V}$)
* $\alpha$: Firing delay angle ($\text{radians}$)
* *Meaning*: Calculates voltage delivered to a resistive load based on TRIAC turn-on delay.


* **RC Snubber Time Constant ($dv/dt$ protection)**:
$\tau = R_S C_S$
* $\tau$: Time constant ($\text{s}$)
* $R_S$: Snubber resistance ($\Omega$)
* $C_S$: Snubber capacitance ($\text{F}$)
* *Meaning*: Dampens high-frequency voltage spikes across MT1/MT2 to prevent false triggering.


* **Snubber Resistor Minimum Value ($di/dt$ protection)**:
$R_S \ge \frac{V_{PEAK}}{I_{TD}}$
* $V_{PEAK}$: Maximum transient voltage ($\text{V}$)
* $I_{TD}$: Maximum allowable turn-on current of TRIAC ($\text{A}$)
* *Meaning*: Limits the initial discharge current of the snubber capacitor into the TRIAC.



**Practical Use**

* **TRIAC + DIAC**: Standard domestic incandescent light dimmers, variable speed ceiling fans.
* **GTO**: Electric locomotive traction motor drives, high-power static VAR compensators.
* **LASCR**: High Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) transmission line optical triggering (immune to immense electrical noise).
* **Solid State Relays (SSR)**: TRIAC driven by an optoisolator for completely silent, spark-free AC switching.

**Design Trade-Offs**

* **TRIAC vs. Back-to-Back SCRs**: Back-to-back SCRs offer much higher $dv/dt$ rating and higher current capacity but require complex, isolated dual-gate drive transformers. TRIACs are cheaper and need only one gate drive but suffer from slower commutation and higher false-triggering risks in inductive loads.
* **GTO Gate Drive**: Eliminates forced commutation capacitors/inductors but requires massive, expensive gate drive amplifiers to deliver the high $\beta_{OFF}$ reverse current.
